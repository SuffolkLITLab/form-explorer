What is the Certificate of Dissolution used for? 

Like  your  divorce  decree  (Findings  of  Fact,  Conclusions  of  Law,  Judgment  and  Decree),  the 
Certificate of Dissolution signed by a judge can be used to prove that you are divorced.  They can 
also be used to prove that a person’s name was  changed in the divorce  case.  The Certificate of 
Dissolution contains far less personal information, and is much shorter, than the divorce decree.  See 
Minn. Stat. § 518.148. 

Can the Certificate of Dissolution be used to transfer title to real estate? 

No.  The Certificate of Dissolution does not show proof that title of real estate is transferred from 
one party to another.  A different form called a Summary Real Estate Disposition Decree may be 
used to show an award of real estate between the parties.  See Minn. Stat. § 518.191 (NOTE: The 
Minnesota Judicial Branch does not publish a Summary Real Estate Disposition Decree.  You may 
be able to find a sample form at a law library, a legal publisher, and the Minnesota Department of 
Commerce website.).   

Do you have all the information you need to fill out the forms? 

The Certificate of Dissolution form (DIV103) asks for the following information (see Minn. Stat. § 
518.148): 

•  The county, judicial district, and court file number of your case; 
•  Current name and former name(s) of each party;  
•  Date the judge or referee signed the Judgment and Decree; and 
•  Details about name changes, if any, in the divorce case.

Getting the Certificate of Dissolution (DIV103) Signed by a Judge 

Step 2 

f you are not already divorced, the court will either prepare the Certificate of Dissolution to be attached 
to the judgment and decree at the time of granting the dissolution of marriage or direct an attorney, or one 
of the parties if both parties are self-represented, to prepare and submit the Certificate of Dissolution to 
the court.   

If you are already divorced (the Judge has already signed your Findings of Fact, Conclusions of Law, 
Judgment and Decree, and the court administrator has entered the judgment) and a Certificate of 
Dissolution has not been prepared or previously filed with the court, you may make a written request and 
the court shall approve the request.  The court may require you or your attorney to prepare the Certificate 
of Dissolution and submit the Certificate to the court.
