FOURTH COURT OF APPEALS 

INTERNAL OPERATING PROCEDURES 

FOR THE HANDLING OF CASES 

Applicability 

Seniority 

“By Lot” 

These Internal Operating Procedures for the Handling of Cases apply to all civil 
and  criminal  proceedings  filed  in  the  Fourth  Court  of  Appeals  except  appeals 
arising  under the Parental  Notification  Act,  which are  governed by  that  Act and 
sections 2, 4, and 16 of these rules only. 

The chief justice is the senior justice. The seniority of a justice is determined by the 
date the justice joined the court. If two or more justices join the court on the same 
date, their seniority relative to one another will be determined by lot. 

Whenever these rules provide that assignment shall be “by lot,” the responsibility 
for making the assignment by lot is entrusted to the clerk of the court. In making an 
assignment by lot, the clerk of the court may decide the procedure to be employed. 

“Justice” and “Participating Justices” 

When “justice” and “justices” is used in these rules (other than in section 2), it refers 
both to the court’s chief justice and justices. 

A “participating justice” is a justice (including any visiting justice assigned to the 
case) who is not recused or disqualified from considering whether to grant en banc 
consideration or reconsideration or participating in en banc review in a particular 
case. 

Court Records 

a. 

Public Records 

Unless made confidential by law, the court’s opinions, judgments, and orders are 
public records. The original briefs and other papers filed in a case, clerks’ records, 
and reporters’ records are public records unless sealed by order of the trial court in 
accordance with Rule 76a of the Texas Rules of Civil Procedure, by another court 
in accordance with other law, or by other law.

Failure to Pay Filing Fee 
 
 

If a document is tendered for filing without the appropriate filing fee, notice will be 
sent to the filer.  If the filing fee is not paid within ten days after the date notice is 
sent, the staff attorney assigned to the case will be notified. 

Alternative Dispute Resolution  

Local Rule 2 provides that “[o]n a party’s motion, or on the Court’s own initiative 
after  reviewing  the  docketing  statement,  the  Court  may  refer  a  civil  case  to 
alternative dispute resolution.”  On the motion of any party, the Court may facilitate 
referrals of civil cases to alternative dispute resolution. 
 

 

 

 
Assignment of Monitoring Justice 
 
When an ordinary appeal (which includes a restricted appeal for purposes of these 
Internal  Operating  Procedures)  or  non-parental  termination  accelerated  appeal  is 
filed, it must be assigned to a justice in rotation in order of seniority to monitor.

b. 

Definitions Used during Monitoring 

In ordinary appeals and non-parental termination accelerated appeals, the justice to 
whom an appeal is assigned for monitoring purposes is the “monitoring justice”; 
the monitoring justice’s staff attorney is the “monitoring attorney”; the “monitoring 
panel” is the monitoring justice and the next two justices in order of seniority who 
are not recused or disqualified; and the “monitoring period” is the period of time 
beginning with the date the appeal is filed and ending on the date that a submission 
panel is assigned to the appeal in accordance with section 9.  In parental termination 
appeals, the  “monitoring justice” is the authoring justice assigned in accordance 
with section 9.c; the “monitoring attorney” is that justice’s staff attorney assigned 
to the case; the “monitoring panel” is the panel assigned in accordance with section 
9.c; and the “monitoring period” is the period of time beginning with the date the 
appeal is filed and ending on the date that the appeal is submitted.

Briefs 

When a party’s brief is filed or an amicus brief is received, the fact of its 
filing or receipt must be entered into the court’s case management system 
and  the  brief  delivered  to  the  monitoring  attorney  as  soon  as  practicable

After receiving a brief, the monitoring attorney must review it to ensure that 
it complies with Rule 38 of the Texas Rules of Appellate Procedure.

Appellate Procedure may be issued by the monitoring panel. 

When the response brief in an ordinary appeal has been filed or the time for 
filing a response brief has expired, the monitoring attorney must review the 
briefs to determine whether oral argument has been properly requested by 
any party and, if so, whether to recommend that the appeal be submitted 
with oral argument or on briefs in accordance with the criteria set forth in 
section 11.a.iii.

ADMINISTRATIVE ORDER 

1.  SCOPE—This order governs notices of late record and extensions of time to file records 
and briefs in ordinary, restricted, and accelerated appeals, except for “an appeal of a suit 
for  termination  of  the  parent-child  relationship  or  a  suit  affecting  the  parent-child 
relationship filed by a governmental entity for managing conservatorship.”  See TEX. R. 
JUD. ADMIN.  6.2,  reprinted  in  TEX. GOV’T CODE ANN.,  tit.  2,  subtit.  F  app.,  (a  parental 
termination appeal).  

2.  DUE DATE—For purposes of this order, the “due date” for records and briefs is the date 
on which the record or brief was initially due under Texas Rule of Appellate Procedure 
35.1 (civil records), 35.2 (criminal records), or 38.6 (briefs).  
 

3.  NOTICE OF LATE RECORD 

.  If a record is not received by the due date in a civil case, the Clerk must send to the 
responsible official a “notice of late record.”  See TEX. R. APP. P. 37.3(a)(1).  If a record 
is not received by the due date in a criminal case or a parental termination appeal, the 
Clerk must refer the matter to the court.  See TEX. R. APP. P. 37.3(a)(2). 

B.  The  Clerk  must  send  the  notice  required  by  Texas  Rule  of  Appellate  Procedure 
37.3(a)(1) no later than five days after the date the record was due in a non-parental 
termination appeal. 

.  The notice required by Texas Rule of Appellate Procedure 37.3(a)(1) must advise the 
responsible official of the date the record was due and ask the official to file either (i) 
a Notification of Late Record, explaining the delay within ten days of the date of the 
notice or (ii) the record within thirty days of the date of the notice in an ordinary or 
restricted appeal and within ten days of the date of the notice in an accelerated appeal.  
See TEX. R. APP. P. 37.3(a)(1).

4.  EXTENSIONS OF TIME 

A.  Records—The Clerk is authorized to attest to per curiam orders granting extensions of 
time to file a record until thirty days after its due date in ordinary and restricted appeals 
and until ten days after its due date in non-parental termination accelerated appeals.  If 

 

 

 

 

the motion requests an extension of time for a period in excess of thirty days in ordinary 
and restricted appeals and in excess of ten days in non-parental termination accelerated 
appeals, the Clerk is authorized to attest to a per curiam order granting the extension of 
time, in part, for thirty days after the record’s due date in ordinary and restricted appeals 
and  for  ten  days  after  the  record’s  due  date  in  non-parental  termination  accelerated 
appeals.  The Clerk may not otherwise attest to per curiam orders extending the time to 
file  a  record  and  must  refer  all  further  extension  requests  to  the  monitoring  staff 
attorney and justice.  

B.  Briefs—The Clerk is authorized to attest to per curiam orders granting extensions of 

time to file a brief according to the following procedures: 

a.  First motions for extension of time that are not actively opposed: The Clerk is 
authorized to attest to per curiam orders granting a first motion for extension of 
time until thirty days after the brief’s due date in ordinary and restricted appeals; 
if the motion requests an extension of time for a period in excess of thirty days, 
the Clerk is authorized to attest to a per curiam order granting the extension of 
time,  in  part,  for  thirty  days  after  the  brief’s  due  date.    In  non-parental 
termination accelerated appeals, the Clerk is authorized to attest to per curiam 
orders granting a first motion for extension of time until twenty days after the 
brief’s  due  date;  if  the  motion  requests  an  extension  of  time  for  a  period  in 
excess of twenty days, the Clerk is authorized to attest to a per curiam order 
granting the extension of time, in part, for twenty days after the brief’s due date.  
All opposed motions are sent to the monitoring staff attorney. 
 

b.  Second motions for extension of time that are not actively opposed: The Clerk 
is  authorized  to  attest  to  per  curiam  orders  granting  a  second  motion  for 
extension  of  time  until  sixty  days  after  the  brief’s  due  date  in  ordinary  and 
restricted appeals, with the approval of the monitoring staff attorney.  In non-
parental termination accelerated appeals, the Clerk is authorized to attest to per 
curiam orders granting a second motion for extension of time until forty days 
after the brief’s due date, with the approval of the monitoring staff attorney.  All 
per curiam orders granting a second motion for extension of time will include 
the language, “Further requests for extension of time will be disfavored.”  All 
opposed motions are sent to the monitoring staff attorney.
