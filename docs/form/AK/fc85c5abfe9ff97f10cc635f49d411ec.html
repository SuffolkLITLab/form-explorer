INSTRUCTIONS:   
HOW TO ASK THE COURT TO APPOINT A GUARDIAN FOR AN ADULT 
 
Introduction 

Appointing a  guardian  for a person is  a serious  matter.   It  takes away the person’s freedom  to 
make many of the important decisions in the person’s life.  Appointing a guardian should not be 
done unless there is no other alternative. 

These  instructions  talk  about  the  petition  for  a  full  guardian  (form  PG-100),  but  most  of  the 
information  applies  to  the  petition  for  a  partial  guardian  (form  PG-103)  and  the  petition  for  a 
conservator (form PG-104) as well.  If these instructions conflict with the particular petition form 
you are filling out, follow the instructions on the petition form. 
 

Lawyers and Representing Yourself 

Before  starting  this  process,  it  may  help  to  talk  to  a  lawyer  who  is  familiar  with  guardianship 
procedure.   If  you are unsure about whether  you should hire a lawyer,  many lawyers will do a 
free or low-cost consultation for a short period of time to help you decide if it is a good idea to 
have legal help in your case.  If you do not know a lawyer, you can call or write:

Definitions of Some Terms Used in These Instructions1 
Incapacitated Person  A  person  whose  ability  to  receive  and  evaluate  information  or  to 
communicate decisions is impaired.  This impairment is so extensive that 
the person is not able to provide at a basic level for the person’s physical 
health or safety (healthcare, food, shelter, clothing, personal hygiene, and 
protection) without court-ordered assistance. 
The person  who  starts  the case and  signs the petition asking the court to 
appoint a guardian. 
The person who is alleged to be incapacitated and in need of a guardian. 
A person who has a guardian or conservator appointed by the court.  Thus, 
the “respondent” is called a “protected person” after the judge appoints a 
guardian or conservator for the person. 
Another term (or synonym) for “protected person” that you might see on 
some  forms  or  websites.    “Ward”  is  considered  a  less  modern  term  for 
adults with  guardians, but  is  still  currently used  in  the Alaska statutes to 
describe persons under guardianship.

To  ask  the  court  to  appoint  a  full  guardian  for  an  adult,  fill  out  the  Petition  for 
Appointment  of  a  Full  Guardian  for  an  Adult  (form  PG-100).    The  instructions  for 
this form begin on page 7. 

To ask the court to appoint a partial guardian for an adult, fill out form PG-103.  A 
partial  guardian makes decisions on limited issues, like housing, medical  care, legal 
issues, finances, or personal services.  This is different from a full guardianship where 
the guardian makes decisions on all of those issues, but  the  court  process  is  largely 
the same.  In a partial guardianship, you only need to show that the respondent needs 
help in the areas where you are asking the partial guardian to have decision authority.   

To  ask  the  court  to  appoint  a  conservator  for  an  adult,  fill  out  form  PG-104.    A 
conservator  makes  decisions  about  the  protected  person’s  financial  affairs  (money 
and  property),  but  not  other  areas  of  the  person’s  life.    A  conservator  could  be 
appointed because a person is incapacitated (as required for a guardian), but there are 
also  other  reasons  that qualify, such  as  when the protected person has  a physical  or 
legal  barrier  preventing  access  to  accounts  or  important  documents  (see  AS 
13.26.401).  The procedure for appointing a conservator is similar, but not identical, 
to appointing a guardian. 

If  this  is  an  emergency,  also  fill  out  the  Emergency  Petition  (form  PG-101)  for 
appointment  of  a  temporary  guardian.    See  form  PG-525  for  more  detailed 
instructions about the emergency petition.   

Note:  A  situation  qualifies  as  an  emergency  only  if  the  respondent  needs 
immediate services to protect the respondent against serious injury, illness, or 
disease, and the respondent is not capable of getting the necessary services on 
the  respondent’s  own.    The  emergency  appointment  is  temporary  and  the 
guardian is only authorized to get those specific emergency services.2  If your 
situation  does  not  qualify  under  this  legal  definition  of  emergency,  you  can 
still  ask  the  court  to  hear  the  petition  more  quickly  by  filing  form  PG-108, 
Motion for Hearing on Shortened Time under Probate Rule 14.1.  You must 
explain  why  the  court  should  expedite  your  case  and  not  follow  the  normal 
timeline.

Visitor’s Investigation and Expert’s Report. 

Within 90 days  after the petition  is filed, the visitor must file an evaluation report10 
with  the court and give a copy of it to  the respondent,  to  the respondent’s attorney, 
and to you (the petitioner).  In order to prepare this report, the visitor must investigate 
the respondent’s current situation, including the respondent’s present living situation 
and financial resources. 

The  visitor  must  give  a  copy  of  your  petition  to  the  respondent  and  explain  what  it 
means.    The  visitor  will  tell  the  respondent  about  the  respondent’s  rights,  including 
the  right  to  talk  to  an  attorney  (and/or  an  expert  in  the  field  of  the  respondent’s 
alleged incapacity) before answering any of the visitor’s questions.  The visitor will 
tell  the  respondent  how  to  contact  the  court-appointed  attorney  and  must  help  the 
respondent make contact if the respondent asks for help. 

The  visitor  will  then  ask  the  respondent  questions  to  determine  the  respondent’s 
ability  to  care  for  the  respondent’s  self  and  the  types  of  help  the  respondent  needs.  
The visitor will also interview you (the petitioner) and other people who can provide 
information needed for the visitor’s report.

Court Hearing.12  

The hearing must be held within 120 days after you file your petition unless the court 
postpones the hearing for cause.13  For example, the court might need to postpone the 
hearing if additional medical examinations for the respondent are needed. 

The respondent has the right to be present at the hearing.  Usually these hearings are 
closed to the public, but the respondent has the right to choose to have it open to the 
public.  Usually the hearing is before a judge or master alone, but the respondent has 
the right to choose a jury (instead of a judge) to decide the issue of incapacity . 

At the hearing, the respondent has the right to present evidence and to ask questions 
of any witness.  The respondent can remain silent (and not answer any questions) if 
the respondent wishes to.   

(1) the respondent is an “incapacitated person” (as defined on page 1), AND

Costs in Guardianship Proceedings 

Cost 

 

 

Who Must Pay 

 

iling Fee 

The petitioner must pay the filing fee according to Administrative 
Rule 9(b)(2)(G)  (unless the court waives the fee because the 
petitioner is indigent).18   

ttorney for Petitioner 

The petitioner must pay for the petitioner’s own attorney.  It is not 
required for the petitioner to have an attorney. 

ttorney for Respondent 

(incapacitated person)  
 

The respondent must pay if financially able to afford the attorney. 
 
If the respondent cannot afford an attorney, the court will appoint 
OPA to represent the respondent, and the state will pay for it.19  

ourt Visitor 

xpert 

econd Expert Requested 

uardian Ad Litem  

If the court finds that the petitioner started a case that was 
malicious, frivolous, or without just cause, the court may require 
that the petitioner pay some or all of the costs of the respondent’s 
attorney.20 

The court will pay for the costs of the court visitor, unless the 
petition is malicious, frivolous, or without just cause (in which 
case, the petitioner must pay).21 

The state will pay for the costs of the expert appointed by the court 
under AS 13.26.226(c), unless the petition is malicious, frivolous, 
or without just cause (in which case, the petitioner must pay).22 

The respondent must pay if the respondent asks the court to 
appoint another expert to examine the respondent and testify for 
the respondent at the hearing.  However, if the respondent is 
financially unable to pay this expert, the state will pay.  If the court 
decides the case is malicious, frivolous, or without just cause, the 
court can order the petitioner to pay.23 

If the court appoints a Guardian Ad Litem, the respondent must 
pay.  If the respondent cannot afford to pay, the state will pay.  If 
the proceeding is malicious, frivolous, or without just cause, the 
court can order the petitioner to pay.24
