NON -PRIMARY CARE PROVIDERS WITH VERY LOW INCOMES When the non primary care providerÕs income is very low, different rules for calculating child support apply. If the annual gross income of the non primary care provider is less than the federal poverty guidelines, the non primary care providerÕs weekly parental support obligation may not exceed 10% of the non primary care providerÕs weekly gross income, regardless of the amount of the partiesÕ combined annual gross income. No additional amounts such as health insurance, child care, or extraordinary medical expenses should be added to this total. Please note this amount is calculated assuming a household of one person. If the non primary care providerÕs annual gross income, without adjustments, is in the self support reserve for the total number of children for whom support is being determined, the amount listed in the self support reserve multiplied by the number of children is the non primary care providerÕs basic support obligation for the children, regardless of the partiesÕ combined annual gross income. The non primary care providerÕs proportional share of childcare, health insurance premiums, and extraordinary medical expenses is added to this basic support obligation. This paragraph does not apply if its application would result in a greater support obligation than a support obligation determined without application of this paragraph. If the non primary care providerÕs income falls in the self support reserve (the shaded area of the Child Support Table) , check the box in the non primary care provider column next to Òself support reserveÓ on the child support worksheet. If the non primary care providerÕs income is lower than all income amounts listed in the Child Support Table, check the box next to Òbelow poverty levelÓ on the child support worksheet.
